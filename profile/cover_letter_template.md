# Cover Letter Style Guide

## Who I am (agent context)
Aidan Kuo — recent grad (June 2025, University of Washington), B.A. Geography: Data Science,
Minor in Informatics. Background in data analytics, Python, SQL, and building data-driven apps.
Based in Houston TX. Looking for entry-level data analyst or junior software/data engineering roles.

## Tone
- Confident but not arrogant — I'm a recent grad, not pretending otherwise
- Direct and specific — no filler phrases
- Conversational but professional — not stiff or corporate
- Avoid buzzwords: "passionate", "rockstar", "ninja", "synergy", "leverage", "fast-paced environment"

## Structure (always follow this order)
1. Opening (1–2 sentences): Name the exact role. Add ONE specific detail about the company
   that shows genuine research — a product, a recent initiative, a tech stack they use.
2. Experience paragraph (3–4 sentences): Highlight the most relevant project or skill for THIS
   specific role. Lead with the outcome, not the tool. Include at least one number if possible.
3. Fit paragraph (2–3 sentences): Briefly connect my background to what they're looking for.
   Mention geography/geospatial background if the role involves location data or mapping.
   Mention AI/NLP project if the role involves ML, LLMs, or data pipelines.
4. Close (1–2 sentences): Simple, direct call to action. Express interest in a conversation.

## Length
- 250–350 words maximum
- 4 paragraphs maximum
- Short is always better than long

## Always include
- The exact job title from the listing in the opening line
- One concrete achievement with a metric (e.g. "reduced manual analysis time by 80%",
  "99% uptime", "25% faster results", "10,000+ entry dataset")
- One specific detail researched about the company — never generic

## Never include
- "I am writing to apply for the position of..."
- "I have always been passionate about data..."
- "I am a quick learner and a team player"
- More than one paragraph about education
- A list of tools/languages (that's what the resume is for)
- Salary expectations
- References to being a recent graduate or having limited experience
  (frame everything positively — projects ARE real experience)
